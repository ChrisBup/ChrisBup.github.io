CUSCO AND THE INCA TRAIL

OUR TRIP

¡Hola! This is the travel log of Chris Heuberger (that's me). I just spent 10 days in Peru with mi esposa, Jillian Russo, and had too much to share for a mere Facebook post so I thought I'd collect my photos, sketches, and notes here for anyone interested. It's way too much, I know, but I also wanted as full a record as possible of our trip because this was one of the most amazing travel experiences I've ever had and I don't know of much else that could come close. So if you're looking to go yourself or are just curious, enjoy!

Our visit broke down to four days in Cusco, four days hiking the Inca Trail, and two days in Ollantaytambo (Oy-yan-tie-TAHM-boh), a small town on the way back to Cusco.

First, some general impressions: Peru is an engaging and charming place packed with fascinating Inca history and simply astounding Inca ruins everywhere. Cusco is accommodating to travelers from all over the world but not touristy per se—there were no tourist traps to speak of. And Peruvians are extremely welcoming, relaxed, and thankfully, very forgiving of tourists. I was saying "muchas gracias" a lot which is good because it's basically the only Spanish I know.

An enduring theme of our visit was that Peruvian culture is a blend of two immensely old, major civilizations built on top of each other: Inca and Spanish. In cathedrals of Cusco, elements of catholicism are combined with traditional Andean beliefs so for example, paintings of the Virgin Mary depict her in the shape of a mountain, with a snake, or under a moon. There's also a painting of the Last Supper with cuy (guinea pig, a local delicacy) presented hooves up on the table.

And that brings me to another highlight: Peruvian food. It is amazing. We enjoyed ceviche, guinea pig, and alpaca as well as extraordinary dishes like tacu tacu, ají de gallina, and chupe de verduras. The main restaurants or pubs we ate in, all of which we recommend, were Limo (superb ceviche and the best pisco sour in town), Casa Qorikancha (housed in a Spanish colonial palace), Cross Keys Pub (have a Peruvian microbew on the balcony), Papachos (for a burger), Jack's Cafe (incredible huevos rancheros breaki), Greens Organic (vegetarian with everything grown in the Sacred Valley and another amazing balcony), Norton Rat's Tavern (a fun crowd assembled here to watch the Peru/Brazil Copa America futbol game—let's not talk about how that went), and the Inka Grill on the Plaza de Armas (try the empanadas).

Some good coffee was served at some breakfast spots but the primary eye-opener in the Andes is coca tea. It's served all day, everywhere and is even meant to help with altitude sickness. Put a few whole dried leaves in hot water or chew up a few to hold in your cheek for a steady taste while you hike.

Cusco's own cerveza is called Cusqueña but the drink to order is a pisco sour. Sometimes, you'll see chilcano on the menu which is a take on the pisco sour, sort of like a margarita, and quite delicious.

Indeed, our main activities in Cusco seemed to be eating excellent food on balconies overlooking plazas and constantly petting llamas and alpacas—both are all over the city. Here's the difference between llamas and alpacas: llamas are larger and have long, banana shaped ears. They are more like camels and are bred as pack animals. Alpacas are smaller and poofier, usually with wool coming over their forehead and eyes. They are more like sheep, with smaller ears than llamas and are bred for their wool and meat. There are also two types of wild llamas, the guanaco and the vicuña, which is the national animal of Peru and is on their flag.

And all of this exists in one of the most remote spots on earth, both naturally and sort of literally, breath taking. Cusco was the Inca capital and holy city and is situated very high up in the Andes, covering a sprawling valley. The name means naval because it was thought to be the center of the world which explains why all roads seem to lead to Cusco. Another fun fact: the flag of Cusco is rainbow-striped, pretty much exactly like the gay pride flag and is delightfully flown just about everywhere.

Our first interaction with a Peruvian was Pedro, whom I sat next to on the plane. We started talking and when he learned Jillian and I were on our honeymoon but the airline had changed our tickets so we weren't sitting together, he called for the flight attendant, spoke to her in Spanish, and arranged to give up his seat so that Jillian could take it. Pedro, wherever you are, you were an excellent ambassador for your country.

Our cab driver, Mauricio, was a wealth of information, both official and otherwise. En route to our hotel, we had front row seats for a middle-of-the-street fist fight that broke out between two other taxi drivers. Both men ended up with a bloody nose before someone broke it up. That was the one and only moment of aggression or hostility we witnessed our entire trip.

Our hotel, El Balcon, was awesome, with a beautiful garden courtyard, excellent views of the city, and coca tea available in the common room day or night. Our second day, we even had a humming bird hovering around the flowers on our table at breakfast. It was a little too much. There was a couple staying there as well, from England, who had been there three times before and was happy to give us tips on acclimatizing and the like.

Every morning, I got up early for a run around town. I recommend this technique for any traveler. It's the best way to see a new city close up before everyone wakes in the morning. You can cover a lot of ground running and explore all those hidden alleys, inviting gardens, and daunting stairways too—of which Cusco has plenty. Bring a small camera and keep it in one hand as you go and you'll get plenty of nice shots. One morning I even wandered into Sacsayhuaman, an important Inca archeological park. I didn't realize where I was until I had run to the top of a mountain and noticed that I had inadvertently snuck into a historic site! I would return with Jillian later that day, now an expert on where to go, and more importantly, a paying visitor.

Sacsayhuaman (Sack-sah-wah-MAHN) is a gigantic walled structure above Cusco, famous for the size of the carved stones in the wall and has absolutely the best views of the city. Do it.

Our second day, there was a parade in the main square, Plaza de Armas. We hit the huge local mercado and found some kind of llama sanctuary in the middle of the city too. We took roughly one thousand photos and I finally got to pet a llama! This was also game day. Cienciano, the local club in the premier division was playing Ayacucho at home, in the Estadio Inca Garcilaso de la Vega. It was an important match too because the struggling Cienciano was in danger of being relegated to the lower league if they couldn't eke out some wins at the end of the season and Ayacucho was in the same situation so a win would be big. This by the way, is a practice I think American sports should adopt. Make room for minor league teams to try their luck in the majors by relegating the lowest three or four teams at the end of each season. In this case however, the result was not definitive. The game ended in a 1-1 tie. A ten soles (around $3) Cienciano hat helped me fit in as a supporter while saving me from further sunburn but the coolest part was actually buying the tickets. To do that, you walk along the outside of the stadium until you see people lining up at, literally, a hole in the wall where a single brick had been removed. This is the ticket office. Put your money down (25 soles) and hope that it's all legit. With tickets in hand and an hour before kickoff—and after playing a little pelada (street soccer) with the locals—we went to a bar in the shadow of the stadium and enjoyed our first Cusqueña. In the stadium, I had some street food and my first chicha, a corn beer that is sold anywhere with a red flag flying outside. Although it ended in a tie, it was a thrill to cheer, "Sí, se puede" for the home squad.

Then it was time for the Museo de Arte Pre Columbino (The Museum of Pre-Columbian Art) which absolutely blew me away. We ended up returning here the next day, it was so cool. It was astounding how old the objects and art in there were but also how aesthetically appealing they still are to modern tastes. Lots of bold geometric patterns, simplified anthropomorphic creatures, clever use of form and medium, and brilliant graphic styles. And so many tattoo ideas.

We also went to the Museo Korikancha (Kor-ree-CAHN-chah; although sometimes spelled with a "Q") on our third day which is a gigantic Spanish monastery and cathedral built on the original site of the primary sun temple in the Inca religion. All the churches in Cusco were built on the sites of Inca palaces and temples which is crazy because they are everywhere.

My fourth day started with a long walk to the few neighborhoods in Cusco I felt I had left to explore. But by the time I returned to the hotel, my stomach wasn't feeling so stellar. I spent the rest of the day—the day before we were supposed to hike the Inca Trail—sick in bed, nauseous, and with a fever. We think it was food poisoning with the culprit likely being ice that had been frozen from tap water that had melted in a drink or lettuce or tomato that had been washed in tap water. I knew not to drink water from the faucet but I was not careful enough not to brush my teeth with it or eat anything that had come in contact with it. Let this be a lesson, fellow travelers. You don't want to be throwing up the day before a treacherous, four-day hike, trust me.

Thankfully, it was a 24-hour bug and cleared up by 4am the next day, when we had to catch our bus for the trek. More on our Inca Trail experience below.

The other things I wish I'd done differently? Buy or print my own map of Cusco. Wifi is not happening (embrace it) and the free maps you accumulate from hotels are often difficult to read and never complete. And I'd also brush up a bit more on my Spanish. I was impressed at how easily I got by with just the basics. Most locals speak English but most English-speaking tourists don't even attempt Spanish. Which means with just a few more phrases, I could have given a much better impression and communicated all I needed to in the native tongue.

Upon leaving Cusco, I noted its similarities with San Fransisco—it's extremely hilly and prone to earthquakes. Just before boarding our flight out of the city, we noticed our bench shaking. We looked up and the door and signs were shaking. Then the entire airport was shaking. Some people were beginning to panic. All I could think of was how Inca buildings were made to withstand earthquakes but unfortunately, the Cusco airport was not built by the Incas. We were evacuated on to the tarmac while planes were continuing to touch down. I thought it was cool. Jillian did not. Eventually though, the tremors subsided and we were brought back into the airport and boarded our plane without issue. We learned later that it was a 7.5 on the richter scale but centered further out in the Amazon so we were only feeling the aftershocks. Still, I was psyched to have one last bit of excitement before leaving this wonderful country.

THE INCA TRAIL AND MACHU PICCHU

For our trek, we had a primary tour guide, Flavio, and an assistant guide, Edwin. Both were expert hikers, spoke English well, and could handle all of my many weird questions on Inca history. Flavio especially was awesomely prone to enormous Inca digressions; a history nerd's dream tour guide.

Our group of fellow hikers was a varied crew and super fun bunch: there were the Kiwis, Adrian and Jodene (both from New Zealand but didn't know each other prior); the Canadians: Cari and Jen (both nurses and new friends from Edmonton); Neil from Indianapolis by way of Chicago and St. Louis; the three Brits from East London, Danny, Todd, and Thomas (having just gotten back from the Galapagos and on their way to New Zealand after this I think); and the former Mormons from Provo, Utah—Tyler and Robin (married seven years), and Derrick with his long distance flame Theona (Toni) from Athens, Greece (they had met in school studying biology in Edinburgh). And then there was us, the couple from Brooklyn, New York, neither of whom with a Brooklyn accent.

We all boarded a bus in Cusco in the dark of the early morning and rode through the Sacred Valley via Chinchero, Urubamba, and Ollantaytambo (where we stopped for breakfast). Then it was back on the bus for another half hour to Kilometer 82, the start of the Inca Trail.

We put on sunscreen, fastened our day packs, and set out.

In terms of difficulty, it reminded me of a marathon. If you are young, healthy, and generally fit, you'll be able to do it but you need to be in good physical shape and well-trained if you are going to do it without suffering. If you're not prepared, you may end up going really slow and especially hating that second day. The hike entails over 7,000 feet of cumulative elevation gain and 6,900 feet of descent including more than 3,000 steps. The highest altitude point is at Dead Woman’s Pass at 4,250 meters (13,779 feet) where altitude is an issue. At that elevation, you're short of breath after ascending a flight of stairs. Now imagine way more stairs. But I still think, in part because it was so challenging, it helped me appreciate what we were doing. You can take pride in how you're following in the footsteps of the Inca and every lookout where you can stop to appreciate the phenomenal natural surroundings is a perfect excuse to rest. It's nice to have a sense of accomplishment as if you'd earned your entrance through the Sun Gate on that last day.

And the weather was spectacular. Similar to Cusco, it was pretty warm and sunny during the day and much cooler at night. It rained once, all throughout the second night. Luckily, our tent was quite waterproof and we could enjoy listening to the gush of an intense downpour outside. Thankfully, it did let up at the last minute, around 5:30am, when it was time to hit the trail again. And the rain made for a cool and misty morning, great hiking weather.

Many think that this trail was designed as a pilgrimage following it's own narrative form, complete with twists, turns, cave, and curves, culminating with Machu Picchu as the climax. We certainly passed a bunch of Inca sites along the way that I thought were just as cool as Machu Picchu (just not as big). At each one, we'd stop and our guides would tell us about them. But the truth is, not a great deal is really known about the Incas. So there's lots of speculation and competing theories about what the various sites were primarily used for and what life was like at each one. Although you definitely get the sense that any settlement that still remains was incredibly well-built which meant that it was probably important which probably meant that only scholars, nobles, and royalty lived there.

For anyone interested, here is our day-by-day break down from what I can remember:

DAY ONE

Our hike began in arid terrain, complete with cacti and a hot sun. But pretty soon, we started to ascend. For the better part of the first day and almost all of the second day, it's uphill. Early on, we get some killer views of the snow-capped peak Mount Veronica at 5,860 meters (19,225 feet), the highest peak in the Cordillera Urubamba range. Its Quechua name is Wakay Willca, which means “Sacred Tears.”

I remember saying, "Well at some point, the mountain has to end, right?" to which our guide, Edwin, replied, "Not in the Andes."

Huillca Raccay was built as a strategic look-out point into the Urubamba valley and was likely used to control the Cusichaca valley.

Patallaqta or Llactapata (Quechua for "Elevated Place" or "Upper Town") was an agricultural settlement possibly used to grow and supply food for the inhabitants of Machu Picchu.

Wayllabamba (Quechua for "Grassy Plain") is the last spot on the trail where pack animals are allowed.

Ayapata was our campsite for day one.

DAY TWO

The second day is uphill, sometimes stairs, sometimes just a steep gravely slope, but always uphill. Up and through two cloud forests.

Llulluchapampa was a campsite and rest area for our group.

Warmiwanusca Pass (Dead Woman's Pass) at 4,250 meters (13,779 feet) above sea level was magnificent.

Pacaymayu was our campsite for lunch.

Then more uphill to Runkuracay Pass, believed to have been used as a watchtower over the Pacamayo valley.

Next was a favorite, Sayamarca (Quechua for "Inaccessible Town"). It was built on the end of an impossibly steep and rugged ridge in the middle of an impenetrable valley. It features a beautiful stone staircase and the only place on the trail that I felt my fear of heights kick in because on that staircase, there is a huge rock that juts out, forcing you to the edge and there is no guard rail. You'll want your hands free for that pass.

Chaquicocha was our campsite for day two.

DAY THREE

Phuyupatamarca, (Quechua for "The City Above the Clouds"), they think was an astrological observatory where scholars and astronomers lived and worked. It features an original Inca staircase of 1,000 steps. If your quads aren't burning at this point, a few flights of this bad boy will wake them up.

Then comes Intipata or "Place of the Sun." Lots of llamas at this one.

Wiñay Wayna (Quechua for "Forever Young", the name of a flower that grows in the surrounding area) is a site with 14 fountains or baths and was probably used as a final resting spot and ritual cleansing area before Machu Picchu. The settlement disappears into the Amazon where you can see and hear a nearby waterfall. The mist coming off that waterfall creates especially perfect circumstances to spot a rainbow. And so the site includes what is known as the Temple of the Rainbow, containing 7 windows thought to signify the 7 colors of the spectrum.

The soil in each of its 8-foot wide terraces contained traces of different minerals and modified variations of plants (like slightly different species of quinoa) leading experts to believe that this was sort of a test farm where the Incas experimented on growing coca, corn, medicinal herbs, and other crops in various microclimated terraces along the mountainside. They even tried to breed new plants.

This site was likely built in tribute to the fertility of Pachamama, the earth goddess, so that she would give birth to an abundant crop. The Inca were serious nature worshippers. The agricultural advancements and cultivation practices they developed were so advanced that they seem to have basically eliminated hunger for some time. They knew how to store and preserve food so as to stock up for lean times and travel with it to trade. Dried fish from the coastal areas were traded with coca grown only in the Andes and even yuca, a jungle potato. Coincidentally, this is the day I started to get my appetite back and what was for dinner? Yuca. Oh so good. Shout out to our cook, Sebastian, who whipped up spectacular multi-course meals at every sitting, including some amazing soups, tortilla pizza, quinoa, and meat dishes and all without a kitchen.

DAY FOUR

We woke up at 2:45am, while it was still dark out, because we needed to get to a checkpoint and wait for it to open at 5:30am. A bit of "hurry up and wait."

A couple hours of hiking later we reach the Sun Gate (Inti Punku) at the crest of a pass between mountains and get our first view of Machu Picchu far below.

Edmund Burke had a useful distinction between the beautiful and the sublime. Something that is beautiful, Burke thought, is well-formed, aesthetically pleasing, attractive, and on our scale, like a picturesque garden. Whereas the sublime is vast, overwhelming, exhilarating, threatening in it's power, like standing on the edge of great cliff. When you first see Machu Picchu, that's what you think of. A site that was designed to impress and intimidate in a setting that was spectacular, elaborate, unnerving, and truly awesome. It also would have made the most excellent arena for the ultimate game of hide and seek.

Not only a display of architectural and military might, Machu Picchu must have been hugely religiously significant as evidenced by its Inti Punku (Sun Gate) and Inti Watana (a ritual stone associated with the astronomic clock or calendar of the Inca). With 16 fountains, Machu Picchu also had the highest of royal prestige and is thought to have been a sort of royal estate for Pachacuti where he would go every winter as a peaceful retreat.

One crazy fact we learned: 60% of Machu Picchu is actually hidden beneath the city in the form of an underground filtering and drainage system directing water runoff to minimize erosion and holding the mountain in place by fortifying it's many retaining walls.

The Torreon or "Sun Temple" here is noted for its curved walls and faces east, toward the sunrise so that on the winter solstice in June every year, the first ray of sunlight streams throught the Sun Gate way up on the mount crest and enters far below and squarely through a small window here, landing on a holy rock in the middle of the temple. Due to the change in the earth's axis since Inca times however, the sun passes slightly askew through the window today. That's how precise it was. The Torreon is located directly above the Royal Mausoleum where Pachacuti's mummy once rested.

Where is that mummy now? HE'S RIGHT BEHIND YOU!

They give you the rest of your time there to explore on your own. After that, we drank Cusqueñas at the bar outside of Machu Picchu which was especially dope because Machu Picchu is on the label. And turns out, beer tastes pretty good after a pilgrimage. We then took a bus back to Aguas Calientes, a small town known for its hot springs and a jumping off point for most tourists on their way to Machu Picchu. It's a fun town to explore because it has both a railroad and the magnificent Urubamba River running through it. We ate outside, in front of a restaurant close enough to almost touch the train as it went by our table. There, in Aguas Calientes, we met up with the rest of the crew, and caught the train back to Ollantaytambo.

Ollantaytambo (Oy-yan-tie-TAHM-boh) is the most important site between Cusco and Machu Picchu and the location of the greatest Inca military victory against the Spanish. It's the only town that has kept all of its original Inca layout too. Most streets are cobblestone and have a small canal of water rushing along side it so there are lots of small footbridges jumping over canals in front of every doorway.

While there, we went up to some sprawling ruins of a mighty fortress overlooking the city. Very cool.

The restaurant at our hotel, El Albergue, was sensational. We ate almost every meal there. And if you go out the back, through a hidden gate, and down a long secret path, you can stop by the organic farm, complete with sheep, ducks, and rabbits, where the restaurant sources its food. Go to the second floor at the mercado in this town and there are three juice ladies in the back that will blend you up a delicious jugo or smoothie for a mere three or four soles. Ollantaytambo is a tiny town; quiet, quaint, and perfect to spend two days in, relaxing after a long hike.

INCA HISTORY

Okay, let's get into it: Inca civilization began forming around 1200 AD, but what is known as the Inca empire existed from 1438 until 1572.

The native language of the Incas is called Quechua (KETCH-wah) and is still spoken all over the Peruvian Andes. Although, during Inca times, it was never written down. Today, Quechua spellings have been created to approximate the oral tradition. Streets in Cusco will often have two names, the Quechua and the Spanish.

The Incas had no writing, no money, no iron, and no horses and yet they still created one of the greatest civilizations known to man. In place of writing, they used a form of knot tying called quipu where placement, color, and number of knots denoted meaning. They used stone hammers, bronze chisels, and other ingenious means to form and polish rocks, allowing them to accomplish masterful feats of masonry and stonework that baffle modern engineers.

To communicate across distance, Incas traveled by foot, dispatching messengers known as chaski who would carry quipus via a relay throughout the empire. Each chasqui carried a pututu (a trumpet made of a conch shell to announce departures and arrivals), a quipu in which information was stored, and a quëpi on his back to hold objects to be delivered. Tambos, or relay stations, were constructed at key points along the road system, often consisting of a small shelter with food and water about every 20 kilometers.

Pachacuti (Pah-cha-koo-tee), the Alexander the Great of the Incan empire, ruled from 1438 to 1471 and founded the greatest royal dynasty in Inca history. He launched a huge territorial expansion of the empire in the form of a building program which included the Capac Ñan (the elaborate Inca road system), the Koricancha sun temple in Cusco, and Machu Picchu.

In 1527, Francisco Pizarro, the Spanish conquistador first met the Incas in northern Peru. He would eventually conquer them and name himself the first governor of Peru. Diego de Almagro, one of Pizarro's two original partners, embittered by his small share of the plunder, would start a war against the Pizarros. He lost that war and was executed but his supporters eventually killed Pizarro.

Atahualpa (Ah-tah-WAHL-pah) was the Inca emperor during Pizarro's conquest of Peru. After the sudden death of his father, the emperor strong, he defeated his half brother, Huascar, in a 5-year civil war that devastated the empire and made the Inca susceptible to the Spanish. When captured in 1532, Atahualpa offered a ransom for his own freedom (some scholars believe it was only for his life): two rooms filled with silver and one room filled with gold which the Spanish accepted. However, Pizarro saw his prisoner as a vulnerability so he still had Atahualpa executed in 1533.

Manco Inca Yupanqui (Man-KOH-Een-KAH Yoo-PAN-kee) was then named Inca king, selected by Francisco Pizarro as a puppet ruler in 1533. But Manco soon led a rebellion against his occupiers and fled to Vitcos in 1537, before establishing Vilcabamba in the rain forest as the new Inca capital. During that retreat, he destroyed a number of settlements along the Inca road system to discourage Spanish pursuit.

Manco Inca's favorite wife and queen, Cura Ocllo (Koo-rah-Oh-klo), was executed in 1539, when Vilcambamba was sacked for the first time and Maco Inca himself would be murdered by Spanish refugees at Vitcos in 1544. One of his sons, Sayri Tupac, would rule the Inca state from Vilcabamba until 1560, followed by another son, Titu Cusi who ruled it until 1571.

Tupac Amaru (TOO-pock Ah-MAR-roo), the last of the rebel Inca emperors, was captured by the Spanish in 1572 and beheaded in Cusco's main Square, Plaza de Armas. When the Spaniards sacked Vilcabamba that year, the city was abandoned and reabsorbed into the tropical foliage. Since the Spanish weirdly never recorded the city's location though, it has yet to be rediscovered and thus exists the legend of the Lost City of the Incas.
